**Task:** I needed to understand the conceptual difference between TF-IDF and CountVectorizer to justify my feature engineering choice in Step 4.

**Prompt used:**
> "When is TF-IDF better than CountVectorizer for text classification?"

**What AI suggested:**
AI explained that CountVectorizer counts raw word frequencies, so common words like "company", "year", and "the" get high scores even though they appear in every document and carry no class signal. TF-IDF addresses this by multiplying the term frequency by the inverse document frequency — words that appear in many documents get penalized, so only words that are frequent in one document but rare across others get high scores. 

**What I modified:**
I used this explanation to write my own justification based on the actual dataset and task.

**Why I modified it:**
The assignment requires me to justify feature engineering decisions based on my own results and domain understanding. 

**What I learned:**
I learned that `sublinear_tf=True` in TF-IDF further reduces the weight of very frequent terms by applying a log transform to term frequency, which is particularly useful for ESG text where some terms like "company" or "report" appear constantly across all classes.

**AI errors found:**
None. The explanation was accurate and matched what I observed in my feature comparison results.

**Task:** I needed to understand the two main approaches for handling class imbalance — SMOTE and class_weight='balanced'.

**Prompt used:**
> "What is SMOTE and how does it handle class imbalance in machine learning? How is it different from using class_weight='balanced' in sklearn? Which is better for text classification?"

**What AI suggested:**
AI explained that SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique) generates new synthetic training samples for minority classes by interpolating between existing minority examples in feature space. `class_weight='balanced'` does not change the data at all — instead it adjusts the loss function so that misclassifying a minority class example is penalized more heavily during training, proportional to how underrepresented that class is. AI noted that SMOTE does not work well with sparse high-dimensional data like TF-IDF vectors because interpolating between sparse vectors produces unrealistic synthetic samples. For text classification, `class_weight='balanced'` is the standard approach.

**What I modified:**
I used this explanation to justify my choice of `class_weight='balanced'` and the computed weights printout. 

**Why I modified it:**
I needed to apply the concept to our specific dataset rather than just restating the general definition. 

**What I learned:**
I learned that SMOTE is generally avoided for text classification because TF-IDF vectors are sparse — most values are zero — and interpolating between two sparse vectors does not produce a meaningful "in-between" text. `class_weight='balanced'` is computationally cheaper, works directly with the sparse matrices we already have, and produces comparable or better results.

**AI errors found:**
AI mentioned that SMOTE could theoretically be applied after dimensionality reduction (e.g., on dense embeddings), which is technically true but was not relevant to our use case and could have been misleading. I chose not to include this in my notebook to keep the justification focused.
